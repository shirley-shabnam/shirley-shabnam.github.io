The myth of deinstitutionalization

Excerpted from Stella Akua Mensah and Stefanie Lyn Kaufman-Mthimkhulu

There is a dangerous tale in the United States, one based on a myth of the deinstitutionalization of psychiatric asylums. We are told that the asylum died and is a thing of the past. But those of us who have survived psychiatric incarceration know that not only did the asylum never die — it is, and always was, another prison.

As Hussein Abdilahi Bulhan states in Frantz Fanon: The Revolutionary Psychiatrist, "psychiatry like any therapy should be the meeting of two 'free' people." In our current society, this could not be further from the truth. Every state allows for a person to be involuntarily held for "treatment, observation, or stabilization."

As we talk about prison Abolition — discourse founded and spearheaded by revolutionary Black Women — we must reckon with the history of psychiatry. Both prisons and psychiatric institutions have an overrepresentation of BIPOC, use law enforcement transport, use solitary confinement, forcibly medicate people, and use physical restraints.

Prison culture is not solvable by 'funding the mental health system' more robustly. The mental 'health' system is fundamentally carceral — one of the many kindred systems that function to contain and surveil people, take away their locus of control, and limit their freedom.

If we share a capacity for diverse manifestations of distress and neurology, we must do away with the "sane"/"insane" binary and give each other permission to be free and find healing. The numbers begin to look very different when we account for who is actually being held.

Disability Justice and Restorative and Transformative Justice ask us to imagine the world we want to live in, collectively. We see us taking actual care of each other — not paternalizing and imprisoning each other while referring to this violence as "care."
